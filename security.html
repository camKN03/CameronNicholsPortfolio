SEC-012025

Iran/Israel Conflict, Qatar’s Position and Regional Alignment

Geopolitical briefing · Middle East Council on Global Affairs
June 26, 2025
1,444 words~7 min read

Iran’s 23 June attack on the Al-Udeid airbase in Qatar should be viewed as a symbolic, face-saving retaliation for the U.S. bombing of its nuclear facilities two days earlier. Indeed, the operation was telegraphed and coordinated to allow an evacuation of troops and aircraft to avoid casualties and damage, while also allowing Iran to make a show of force without prompting a major escalation. Tehran appeared to have calculated correctly as President Donald Trump went as far as thanking the Iranians publicly for their indirect coordination and walking down the de-escalatory ladder to the point of announcing a ceasefire between Israel and Iran mere hours later.

At the same time, however, the subtleties of the attack should be considered for their implications. While Al-Udeid is the largest U.S. base in the Middle East and the regional headquarters for USCENTCOM— offering unparalleled balance to the importance of the Iranian nuclear facilities that were struck—an attack on the sovereign territory of Qatar risked both the good standing of the Qatar-Iran relationship and contained a message to the rest of the Gulf. For one, given Qatar arguably has the best relationship in the Gulf with Iran, Tehran likely calculated that it would offer the best chance to manage the political-diplomatic fallout of such an attack.

If, for instance, it had occurred against the UAE, Saudi Arabia or Bahrain, a major rupture in ties would have been likely. Secondly, by choosing a friendly country like Qatar, Iran may have intended to demonstrate that no country in the region is beyond attack should Iran feel it necessary to regionalize/globalize the war in order to put pressure on other countries to bring it to an end.

Security Considerations for Qatar After the Iranian Attack

The attack on Al-Udeid may have posed minimal security risks for Qatar given its well-equipped air defense systems, US support, and Iranian coordination, but perceptions of safety among citizens and residents in the country have been harmed. There may be some economic and social damage resulting from this, as Qatar relies on highly skilled workers to develop its economy and operate complex infrastructure. As those workers are sensitive to safety concerns, the country may incur higher costs in the future to retain talent and remain an attractive destination for working professionals who would prefer to work in more stable environments.

However, field research is needed to better assess the degree to which safety perceptions in Qatar have changed after the attack. While perception changes may be limited and temporary after a single attack, they may be significant and long-lasting if instability persists in the region and/or a second attack takes place. Nonetheless, Qatar can navigate this crisis and mitigate negative externalities by increasing its diplomatic efforts to resolve the root causes of the conflict. It continues to be well-positioned to mediate between all sides, as both President Trump and the Iranian national security council issued statements generously praising the country and its leadership in recent days.

By resolving the crisis swiftly, perceptions of safety in Qatar and the wider Gulf region are likely return to normal levels. Middle East watchers have acknowledged Qatar’s sacrifices and behind-the-scenes efforts to achieve a ceasefire agreement, which was announced shortly after the Iranian missiles were shot down. Formally, however, Qatar reserved its right to respond to Iran in kind, warning against any future infringements on its sovereignty. Gulf partners, including the UAE—with whom Qatar has had a lukewarm relationship in recent years—expressed solidarity with Qatar, sending a powerful message of unity. Qatar could benefit in both the short- and long-term from this development.

Bolstered by the reputational gains from its role in securing a ceasefire between Iran and the US (even if it is tenuous), Qatar could double down on its mediation efforts elsewhere, including in Gaza, Ukraine, and the Horn of Africa. Additionally, the goodwill and solidarity from Gulf partners after the attack could serve as a foundation for further rapprochement, economic integration, and improved diplomatic coordination across the GCC.

Saudi Arabia’s Strategic Role

Following U.S. strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities, the Saudi government engaged in efforts to de-escalate tensions between the United States, Israel, and Iran. Before the American strikes, the Saudi government had already begun talks with Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian, attempting to mediate the conflict. Saudi Arabia also issued a soft condemnation of the U.S. attacks on Iran’s nuclear facilities, cautioning against any violations of Iranian sovereignty. Saudi Foreign Minister Prince Faisal bin Farhan called on the international community to contribute to efforts to enact a ceasefire. The Saudi government also went beyond political action by extending humanitarian assistance to Iran, and providing shelter and aid to Iranians stranded in Saudi Arabia due to the war.

Saudi Arabia is cementing its role as an advocate for regional stability amid an increasingly tense conflict. Marking a notable shift from its earlier Iran policy, Saudi Arabia is effectively encouraging engagement between Iran and the United States, while simultaneously pushing the international community to promote diplomatic values alongside regional powers.

US Interventionism and Implications for Iran

Donald Trump’s decision to bomb Iran’s nuclear facilities was an unprecedented move that risked triggering a dangerous regional war. Given Trump’s previous stance favoring a negotiated prevention of an Iranian nuclear weapons program, as well as his ideological opposition to US involvement in wars in the Middle East, the about-face came as a major surprise. It also demonstrated Trump’s susceptibility to Benyamin Netanyahu’s influence, as has also been observed in Trump’s evolving policy on Gaza. The US intervention, including greenlighting the Israeli surprise attack on Iran that began on 13 June, involved using the Trump administration’s ongoing engagement in nuclear negotiations as a tool of deception against Iran.

This, together with the US strikes on Iran’s nuclear facilities, has likely done damage to America’s international standing, as well as US credibility for Iran—needed for any future agreement. Beyond a few Western capitals that condoned the strike on Iran’s nuclear facilities, much of the world viewed this as a brazen and unlawful assault on Iran’s sovereignty under the pretext of stopping an alleged weapon of mass destruction program—much like the lie that was used to justify the 2003 invasion of Iraq. Indeed, for many Americans, the US operation represented a broken promise from Trump to keep the country out of unnecessary wars in the Middle East, especially among Trump’s “MAGA” base.

In the run up to the strike, the possibility of US intervention split Trump’s Republican party. If the strike had led to a wider war involving the US military, the domestic backlash would have likely grown significantly—a probable reason for why Trump was quick to call for a ceasefire after Iran’s symbolic retaliation. The president needs to avoid the emergence of internal opposition in Congress, as even the smallest rogue faction could affect the ultra-slim Republican majorities in the House and Senate.

Iran’s Future and the Nuclear Question

Iran emerges from the recent conflict debilitated but not beaten. The status of its nuclear program remains uncertain and will determine much about the road ahead. So, too, will be the status of nuclear negotiations and whether they will resume following a ceasefire. Although Iran still wants an end to US sanctions, trust in America’s word has been irreparably damaged by the crisis and it is uncertain if Iranian leaders will be any more flexible on the redline Trump previously set on the country’s ability to enrich uranium. Indeed, many analysts believe Iran now has a greater incentive to exit the Non-Proliferation Treaty and pursue the rapid development of nuclear weapons to insulate itself from future attacks by Israel and the US.

The lack of significant radioactivity around the targeted nuclear sites could indicate that Iran successfully moved at least some of its highly-enriched uranium stockpiles prior to the attacks. However, other reports indicate that there was never a risk of radiation exposure beyond the immediate vicinity of the sites because they are not nuclear power facilities and because “bunker-buster” bombs are designed to collapse the mountain on top of the bunker, which arguably limits radiation from escaping. In the meantime, Iran will probably aim to recover from the war, rebuild air defenses and ballistic missile stockpiles, and possibly even support its regional allies in their own recovery.

It is also likely to turn inward after its security was severely breached by Israeli intelligence and embark on a major counter-intelligence campaign. At the same time, the durability of a ceasefire with Israel is tenuous, and Iran has learned it must always be on guard, even if it was able to demonstrate its ability to forcefully strike back against Israel and penetrate its vaunted air defenses.

SEC-032025

Concept Note: Women in Fragile States

Concept note · women’s education and fragile contexts

The role of education for women in conflict zones and fragile contexts, examining its impact on social fabric, social empowerment, and the construction of national identity.

Introduction and Background

For centuries, women have faced significant barriers to accessing education. Only in recent decades have many countries started offering women equal opportunities to pursue it, and the effects on those states have been consistently positive. Educating women builds human capital for labor markets, raising annual GDP by roughly 1%, lowering conflict risk by about 3%, and strengthening a unified national identity. In fragile states a common thread runs through them all, a lack of equal opportunity for women. Countries that have emerged from fragility, such as Rwanda, also overhauled their government systems to propel women into the workforce. Merely expanding access is insufficient, it must be accompanied by a cultural transformation within these societies.

Education and Opportunities for Women in Crisis Regions (Sahel, Africa, MENA)

Newly created frameworks are helping launch women into higher roles within fragile states by opening up equal opportunities in education. These frameworks help simplify the complex task of integrating half of a country’s population into sectors they never had a chance to enter before.

The 4Es Framework
  1. Empower. Status, representation, and legal reform, with an end to child marriage (roughly 40% of girls marry before 18).
  2. Enhance. Maternal and reproductive health, closing skilled-worker gaps, reducing GBV stigma, and mapping referral systems.
  3. Educate. Overcoming fees, harmful attitudes, early marriage, poor facilities, and the shortage of women teachers.
  4. Employ & Enable. Tackling skill deficits, displacement, capital access, and GBV risk, mainstreaming gender in social-protection and jobs programmes, and expanding agriculture livelihoods.
Created Opportunities for Women
  • Tanzania. A trained counsellor in every school runs life-skills clubs for girls and boys.
  • Nigeria. Female counsellors deliver life-skills training to roughly 340,000 girls in safe spaces.
  • Ghana. The Accountability and Learning Outcomes Project provides gender-sensitive teacher training and guides.
  • Nigeria — AGILE. Conditional cash transfers remove cost barriers for girls.
  • Mali — MIQRA. School feeding and nutrition programmes aimed at girls’ retention and attendance.

How Women’s Education Shapes National Identity and Creates Peace

Rwanda
  • Major constitutional reform to embed gender parity.
  • Girls outperform boys in primary exams and outnumber them across primary and secondary school.
  • Education policy coherence is tightly aligned with national priorities.
South Sudan
  • The 2015 curriculum mainstreams human rights and gender equity so that girls and boys forge a unifying South-Sudanese identity from an early age.
  • It aims to bridge inter-group divides through a shared national narrative.

Potential Scopes for Contribution

Integrating women into more established roles in fragile states, which in turn benefits their economies, is a relatively new effort, which means there is a great deal still to uncover. Many research questions remain open and various projects could be launched to draw attention to girls’ education in these countries. Building the evidence base for integrating women into the education system will undoubtedly help states move away from fragility.

Research Proposals

  1. Curriculum & Cohesion Index.Question Does embedding human-rights content in South Sudan’s 2015 syllabus measurably raise inter-ethnic trust?Contribution Supplies one of the first quantitative links between rights-based curricula and social capital.
  2. Peace Returns.Question What is the marginal reduction in conflict incidents for every added year of female schooling across the Sahel?Contribution Offers evidence for framing education budgets as security investments.

Hypotheses

  1. Education and security elasticity. Each additional year of average female schooling in Sahel provinces predicts a drop of at least 5% in next-year conflict events, holding poverty and governance constant.
  2. Household disjunction. In Rwanda, women Members of Parliament still perform at least two more hours of unpaid domestic work daily than their male counterparts, revealing private-sphere inequality despite public gains.

Rentierism in Saudi Arabia and Qatar

Senior thesis · Dickinson College
Cameron Nichols · Senior Thesis Independent Study · May 7th, 2025
8,691 words~40 min read

The rentier state is an extremely profitable political economic model when done correctly as seen with many states. Two states that have done very well being a rentier state are Qatar and Saudi Arabia. However, even with these wealthy economies, these two countries are trying to find ways to diversify their economy and have more sources of income than just oil from their lands. The world is going through an energy transition. There are more electric cars being manufactured, electric shipping may be right around the corner, and solar farms being planned out. The politics of climate change are becoming more apparent, making powerful countries buying oil from places using more sustainable methods such as carbon capture.

Due to this energy transition throughout the world, the oil market will stagnate around 2035. Which is much sooner than expected. Some countries are not planning for this like Iraq, Iran, Algeria, and Venezuela. These countries may end up having a drastic downfall to their lack of action, something the Arab Gulf States are trying to avoid. Relying on one source of income for your country is not a very sustainable economic decision. You are at the will of how well the market is doing for your one resource making your economy extremely vulnerable to market fluctuations.

If you want a powerful economy and increase economic development, you must diversify your economy. Arab Gulf States like Qatar and Saudi Arabia have a growing young population with rising expectations of jobs and opportunities and the rentier model that usually provides extensive subsidies and government jobs, is becoming much harder to maintain.

With the rentier model comes weak institutions. Rentier states weaker political, economic, social, and legal institutions (Hertog, Steffen. 2010.). They also lack the private sector that is necessary to create a more diversified economy. This does not curate a sustainable economy. For Qatar and Saudi Arabia to move away from the rentier model they must rely on their relationships with two of the great powers of the geopolitical world, The United States of America and China. However, using these two countries for help could ignite a stronger power competition between them which could end up actually halting the Gulf States process of diversifying the economy.

The U.S. may be upset that the Gulf States are seeking help from China. Plus, China building infrastructure there may cause security concerns for the U.S. It could be a similar story to the Huawei wars. The U.S and China have a long history of working with the Arab Gulf States, the U.S. even has some military bases in Qatar and Saudi Arabia. It seems that both countries would be willing to help invest in the Gulf States economies to help them improve their economy. Lastly Sovereign Wealth Funds like Qatar’s QIA and Saudi Arabia’s PIF could help them save and organize their money better to diversify their economy.

These state-owned funds help countries invest in non-oil sectors like real estate, infrastructure, and technology. Arab Gulf States will usually use these funds to invest in foreign markets and companies. This spreads their economy into more markets, making it less vulnerable to market fluctuations.

The rentier state model has been around for a very long time, and it has worked, however it is time for a change, now more than ever. Through the ongoing energy transition through the world, an economy vulnerable to market fluctuations, and a growing young population, many Middle East States must stray away from this political economic model. For Qatar and Saudi Arabia to survive this they must find different ways to diversify their economy and make it stronger or they could become failed states. Using their relations with the United States and China, two great powers in the world, could propel them to be more efficient by staying with their plans and reaching their goals in making a secure economy. By creating more institutions and stronger private sector, and sovereign wealth funds, they could become thriving countries without a countdown towards a stagnant oil-based economy.

Rentierism and Its Qualities

Rentierism is a political economic theory. It helps explain the states that obtain a large amount of their income from rents from a specific resource (“Development of Rentierism.” 2014). Rentier states are less likely to entertain or make industries for their citizens since they do not tax them directly. Meaning weaker institutions. Political, economic, social, and legal institutions are all heavily reduced in a rentier state (Hertog, Steffen. 2010). These four categories help constrict the vagueness of the term “weakened institutions”. It helps specifically to point to what institutions are in fact reduced and in turn makes clear what needs to be worked on to move away from a rentier model.

The lack of effectiveness is what specifically ruins these countries if the rentier political economic theory fails. Though this means that if a rentier state's economic system is improving then they will not feel the effects of these reduced institutions. When it comes to political institutions we see governments, constitutions, and electoral systems all weakened tremendously. Meaning places may not hold elections and if they do, they are a one-party system. They also don’t follow a strict code like a constitution. Both these ideas are minimized and take a lot of power away from the people and give it to the government.

This is why rentier states often either have monarchies by regimes/dictators. The government relies on resource revenues rather than taxation of its citizens, so as long as the government is providing for the citizens needs the government system does not mean much to them. They also have many meaningless jobs in the government, again giving more power to the small group really in charge. The lack of political institutions can make a government susceptible to unhappy citizens if they are not provided enough. There is an unspoken agreement between every government and its people. If they are protected and provided for them in return, they stay ingrained in the society that is built.

However, if the government breaks their end of the bargain, then this could lead to uprisings. When a government relies on one resource for a stable economy, when the market fluctuates so does the bargain. An of example this is Venezuela who has ongoing conflicts with their own citizens throughout many decades. Governments could not give what was needed to their people and so their people moved against them. To move away from the rentier model is to move away from the increased potential of an uprising.

Economic institutions weakened mean systems like markets, property rights, and banking are all weakened too. This can create an unstable economy ready to collapse at any moment due to market fluctuations. It can be very difficult to have any sort of economic development when it comes to an economic system that's foundation is based on one resource. This does not just affect the government but will also affect the citizens too. With this type of institution weakened it also can make it difficult for people to be completely financially independent from the government and grow away from it.

Citizens will become more reliable on a government that's economy could be disrupted at any moment. Another issue under rentierism is the increasing inequality due to the banking system. In Lebanon, individuals who have close ties to powerful political elites own 43% assets in the state's banking system (Dahle, Stephanie. 2020.). The majority of banks in the country also are linked to a political elite (Dahle, Stephanie. 2020.). Economic power is so closely tied to the political leaders. This can lead to bank loans and bonds being given out of political favoritism (Dahle, Stephanie. 2020.).

This creates a system that values the elites more than their own citizens. Even though Lebanon is not a rentier state it shares the same symptom many MENA states share, which is that most institutions are owned or closely connected to the government. The rentier model breeds this type of banking system, especially in Middle Eastern societies. Property rights are very shaky in the rentier model. This is due to the fact that there isn't an assurance of property rights in the first place for its own citizens. Governments can take these properties and if it happens to one property it can and will threaten all properties in that country.

For property rights to be better protected or given in general, there would need to be some sense of democratization (Barma, Naazneen H. 2014.). Which, as stated earlier, is hard to produce in the rentier model.

Social institutions are involved more in the personal lives of the citizens of rentier states. This is why I believe they are the most important institutions to strengthen when fixing the rentier model. This includes systems like family, education, entertainment, and religion. In the rentier model there is less emphasis on the family system since many needs are taken care of by the government. Needs like housing, healthcare, and employment. This is not necessarily a bad thing, but it tends to make people rely less on their family. To add to this, we see a reduced entertainment industry.

For now, this works because the government makes the lives of its citizens easier by giving them everything they need. However, it again can create less of a community in these countries and if these states ever want to move away from this political economic system, they would have to overload this sector to create more jobs and to make its own citizens happier with where they live. A lack of an education system can be catastrophic for the future generations for a country. This could cause a failure in the advancement of technology and science in the state leading them not just to fall behind with a rentier economy but also fall behind from a societal level too.

This would make it nearly impossible for a country to move away from the rentier model due to a lack of innovation. They would have to bring in more citizens from other countries, however, that would effectively lead to them leaving their own citizens behind. Religion is a bit more complex, and this institution seems to actually weaken when trying to move away from rentierism. However, it is not strong to begin with in these types of economies. When looking at vision models for Saudi Arabia there is an emphasis on increasing Islamic teachings throughout the society. This reveals that the state believes under its current status it lacks this institution.

Legal institutions cover courts, law enforcement, and legal codes. Throughout many rentier states we see a lack of fair courts and law enforcement that is for the people. The term that floats around almost all rentier states, especially in MENA is the secret police. The idea completely violates the western idea of police and the justice system. The secret police are exactly what they are called. It is an organization that spies on their own population making sure there is little dissent in the state. If there is too much dissent then people may get silenced, no court or trial.

This is a highly expensive system and only works with a highly massive income. If that income starts to decrease then the systems must become strong by creating fair courts and police that are for protection, not disruption. The state would have to find another way to control their population instead of using hard power on them. Legal codes in rentier states tend to be modified a lot throughout time. This hurts the trust of its citizens but more indirectly affects the amount of investors that support the country. This can hinder the growth of said states (Sullivan, William Curtis. n.d.).

Corruption in the form of cronyism is prevalent in many rentier states and most notable in Middle East rentier states. Cronyism is when people in positions of power choose other people for positions of power based on how close they are with them. This can create a system of government that favors elites that serve the government. It takes power of choice away from the people. It creates a country where the powerful are more incentivized to do what is best for its government and not the citizens in the state. Cronyism is not only contained in the Middle East.

We see it to some extent in every country. Even democracies in northern Europe and North America. However, The MENA region has a much higher level of cronyism, and it affects the citizens much more than other regions. This is all due to the weakened institutions rentierism breeds. The banking system, weakened electoral system, lack of fair courts all contribute to the excess of cronyism in these governments. As stated earlier, the banking system breeds cronyism by elites having close friends own many assets in the banking system. The majority of banking systems are heavily connected to the political elites.

This pushes power towards the elites when it comes to money. The close friends in the banking system also have an incentive to support the government over the people when it comes to the banking sector. The lack of an electoral college in these states also breeds cronyism due to taking the choice away from the people. With a weak electoral system there are no checks on who the political elites put into office, further increasing cronyism. Monarchies further this because the only criteria of holding power is being family. It is seen in Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and the UAE.

These monarchies are not necessarily a bad thing. If the people are content with their leaders, then so be it, however if the people are not content with their leaders it can cause dissent within the state. Some countries, however, are more sinister. Syria for example did hold elections in the past however there would be one party, and the vote would be a yes or no. No would most likely be seen as dissent against the government and would lead to an unfortunate outlook for said person. This leads to my next trait of rentierism that breeds cronyism.

The unfair court system takes the power away from citizens who act like the leaders of their government. When someone may be against the government. They could face consequences and deal with unfair trials; they may have them silenced. This lets political elites get away with putting friends and family in office even if they are not fit for it, even if they are hated by the citizens of the state. Research and development (R&D) is lacking in these rentier states. One major aspect that affects this is the lack of educational institutions in these states.

With a decreased emphasis on education it makes it much harder to improve the research and development sector in these states. They rely so much on their rare resource that they tend to ignore the advancement of other sectors in their society. This tends to make them fall behind in the global stage in technology, medicine, and science. However, with GCC states like UAE, Qatar, and Saudi Arabia that they have a yearn to catch up creating major cities like Doha, Abu Dhabi, and Dubai. However, they are still behind on the global stage playing catch up.

If they keep on track, they could catch up and have a thriving research and development sector. This is one of the most important sectors to improve if a rentier state wants to move away from that political economic system and push towards a more diversified economy. All in all, it starts with the education system. That is what the catalyst is.

The final trait and one of the biggest issues creeping up on these rentier models is high unemployment due to the lack of a job market and high imported labor. They import a lot of labor for the oil market because imported people do not have rights and therefore cannot make demands of the country. These foreign workers are given crappy jobs while citizens are given nice comfortable jobs in the public sector that rarely require real work. Usually this works and everyone stays happy. However, there is a growing young population there with expectations of job opportunities, but government jobs under this system are becoming harder and harder to give out.

This prompts a need to increase the job market in these states. We see this both mentioned in Qatar’s vision plan and Saudi Arabia’s vision plan. By propping up sectors that increase tourism, there will be an increase overall in the job market. However, what is the longevity of this? It has yet to be discovered.

The Need for Economic Diversification

If many rentier states rank among the highest GDP globally, then why do they need to diversify their economy? They have the income; shouldn’t they just use that to fix the other issues that the rentier state causes? Well, there are many answers to this question but the vulnerability to market fluctuations is the overall reason for the need to diversify. Throughout the years the Arab Gulf states have created plans to diversify their economy due to fear of market fluctuations in the oil market. There is Saudi Arabia’s Vision 2030, Qatar’s National Vision 2030, Kuwait’s Vision 2035, Oman’s Vision 2040, Bahrain’s Economic Vision 2030, and the United Arab Emerites’s Vision 2021.

So, why did they keep failing or getting pushed back? One reason is fear of change. When the gulf states have tried to diversify their economy in the past they try to keep the centralized power system unaffected, they were “carefully calibrated to not fundamentally alter the central power dynamics of the prevailing elite bargain” (Arab Political Economy).This avoidance led the states to not “engender economic or political competition” (Arab Political Economy). The leaders of the Gulf States would state how important economic reform is but would then not do what is necessary to create competition.

Elites would still pick the winners and losers in their states, fueling cronyism (Arab Political Economy). It is important to remember that the rentier model, in a sense, is an all or nothing system. The traits attached to the political economic model are ingrained in the system. The gulf states multiple times try to alter the rentier state slightly assuming it will fix their vulnerabilities to market fluctuations but fail every time. To save themselves they must move away from the centralized power dynamic that they have had for many years. However, the reasons for failing their vision plans are not just down to one.

This is just the “common thread” (Arab Political Economy). Why now in 2025, do the gulf states especially the UAE, Qatar, and Saudi Arabia are so fiercely trying to move with their vision plans, megaprojects, and diversify their economy? What changed so recently? The global landscape is changing when it comes to the use of energy. Since being discovered the world has relied heavily on oil, from manufacturing purposes, like shipping and construction to personal uses like travel and appliances. However, this is starting to change. Due to an energy transition, around 2035 the oil market will stagnate and will not increase anymore.

It had been known for a long time that this was going to happen, but no one knew it would occur this soon. The rise in technology for electric vehicles is leading many countries to move away from gas powered cars. Many car companies are starting to make cheaper and more reliable electric cars and in the coming decade they will take over the market. With the rise of technology helping electric vehicles, electric shipping may occur too. This would drastically damage the oil market if successful. Ships, planes, and trains use a vast amount of oil to keep running and if oil is not needed anymore that would lead to a permanent decline in the oil market.

Which again, is the biggest weakness to rentier states under the oil market. There are many countries turning to renewable energy now too. The U.S along with the EU has its own goals with solar, wind, hydro-power, and geothermal technology to help power their electric grid. Yes, climate technology is not where it needs to fully transition but it is progressing towards that. We even see some plans the Gulf States are creating to make their energy needs more renewable. For example, the Al Kharsaah solar farm by QatarEnergy is planned to be a huge step for Qatar turning to renewable sources.

The politics of climate change are becoming more apparent in the geopolitical stage. Some countries will only buy energy resources that are sustainable. This is why carbon capture and storage (CCS) is becoming needed with companies that sell a lot of oil, because it shows an attempt to be more sustainable. There are countries who believe they do not need to change their system nor need any plans to. However, those are not countries whose footsteps you want to follow. Iraq, Algeria, Venezuela are all states that do not have plans on moving away from a rentier model.

On the Fragile States Index funded by the Fund for Peace, Iraq is 31st, Iran is 43rd, Venezuela is 30th, and Algeria is 87th ("Fsi-2024-Rankings" n.d.). Since Venezuela is the low on the list its case study may give insight into what is the end scenario for a thriving rentier state if they do not diversify their economy. Venezuela is descending in a downward spiral politically and economically. The state has one of the richest oil reserves in the world; this led to them having a quick rise in GDP. Mismanagement in the Venezuelan government and U.S sanctions made their downfall harder ("Venezuela: The Rise and Fall of a Petrostate" n.d.).

Iran, Iraq, and Algeria are all slowly following the fate of Venezuela. They are finding other ways to continue surviving but none will lead them to a developed country. Another reason there is a need to move aways from the rentier state is because of the Dutch disease. The Dutch disease was a term first used when the Netherlands found natural gas in the North Sea ("Venezuela: The Rise and Fall of a Petrostate" n.d.). When a country has a large resource that is extremely valuable to the world, many foreign nations will buy from them.

This increases foreign capital and then appreciates the local currency. This in turn “sucks labor and capital” away from other sectors in the country, which makes it difficult to have economic “growth and competitiveness” ("Venezuela: The Rise and Fall of a Petrostate" n.d.). When the export starts to fail then more problems unravel, for example “unemployment rising” ("Venezuela: The Rise and Fall of a Petrostate" n.d.). This is the Dutch disease, and it causes rentier states to become more reliant on their valuable export while simultaneously destroying the rest of their sectors. A diversified economy naturally creates stronger institutions, and these stronger institutions create more jobs.

If a country invests more in other sectors there would be much more growth seen in those sectors. If these sectors grow, then there will be a need for workers in them. There could be the question of whether moving away from the rentier state should happen after creating stronger institutions and weakening cronyism, but they go hand in hand. If you move away from rentierism then you must create stronger institutions because simply investing in these other sectors moves, you away from the Dutch disease which helps move you away from the rentier model.

Qatar and Saudi Arabia have the Dutch disease and luckily, they are pursuing ways to diversify their economy to bolster their economic longevity and development. Both states are not new to this. They both have created plans in the past to diversify their economy showing that Qatar and Saudi Arabia do have some understanding of the situation they are in. However, in the past they seem to have no dire need to diversify, leading them to abandon plans. Now they have no choice but to move forward and are doing whatever they can to get them where they need to be, so their visions are successful.

Qatar has done well with building on their past projects while Saudi Arabia usually leaves abandoned plans abandoned. This has put the countries at different levels in their progress. In the end no matter their past, they will both be better off finding ways to diversify their economy.

Sovereign Wealth Funds

So how do countries like Qatar and Saudi Arabia keep track of the money they save and invest in high growth markets? They use a tool called a sovereign wealth fund. These are government owned funds where money is often collected from a country’s surplus reserves. The main goal of these funds is to save for the future, stabilize the economy, and earn profits to fund government programs. A lot of times they use this fund to invest in infrastructure, real estate, and technology. Both Qatar and Saudi Arabia have their own version of a sovereign wealth fund to help them collect their extra revenue from their oil exports.

Qatar's sovereign wealth fund is named the Qatar Investment Authority (QIA) and Saudi Arabia’s is the Public Investment Fund (PIF). The QIA was created in 2005 by an Amri decision with the goal to diversify the nation's economy beyond its natural resource wealth. Throughout the past 20 years the QIA has invested in many wealthy markets to help prop up other export sectors to help themselves move away from the dutch disease. On the QIA’s website it states its main investment focuses are “technology, media, and telecommunications (TMT), healthcare, retail and consumer, real estate, infrastructure, financial institutions, industrials and materials, liquid securities, and funds” (International Forum of Sovereign Wealth Funds n.d.).

When it comes to assets, Qatar states that it delves into “credit/fixed income, real estate, infrastructure, private equity, public equity, and alternative investments” (International Forum of Sovereign Wealth Funds n.d.). The QIA also has a goal of investing in different sectors in a sustainable way, which is investing with more thought and in hopes of making a positive impact. For the QIA to be effective they must rely on international partnerships meaning they must stay propped up the international stage. The question that must be asked is how does such a small country fight to be noticed among large countries with more variety in export sectors to start with?

Is buying already successful businesses, like they have been doing, enough to stay in the light? The PIF was first established in 1971 by a Royal Decree. In 2015 it was rebooted “when the Kingdom’s Council of Ministers issued Resolution 270, which placed the Fund under the direction of the newly formed Council of Economic and Development Affairs (CEDA)” (International Forum of Sovereign Wealth Funds n.d.). MBS was the chairman of the department that controlled the fund so any decisions on what the fund did had to go through him. The PIF helped create more organizations when finding ways to diversify their economy, by planning out more strategic investments.

The fund has an annual spending budget that is set each year on what it can invest in. The top 3 ways the PIF collects revenue for its fund is through capital injections from the government, government assets transferred to PIF, and loans and debt instruments. Well, what are they investing in? The most important categories they are focused on are food and agriculture, healthcare, metals and mining, real estate, aerospace and defense, automotive industry, and media and technology (International Forum of Sovereign Wealth Funds n.d.). This is a wide array of sectors they are trying to involve themselves in leading to higher job variety and, it is hoped, to a higher success rate when it comes to moving away from the Dutch disease.

Saudi Arabia’s Economic Diversity Strategies

The Kingdom of Arabia is working on a multitude of ways to diversify its economy and achieve high growth in its non-oil sectors. In some cases, they are looking to create entirely new sectors rather than improving on dead weak ones it knows may never actually strengthen. Saudi Arabia knows that strengthening sectors requires important stabilization efforts in the region. Stronger partnerships between neighbors breed more effective dialogue on strategic alliances in the face of a failing political-economic system. Saudi’s mega cities are still in play; however none have been fully built yet. The two most recent places planned were Qiddiya, announced in 2017, and NEOM, announced in 2021.

There is still a chance the places could come to fruition and skyrocket economic diversification into a multitude of sectors. To follow through with Vision 2030, Saudi Arabia must keep the region friendly and not have hostile relations with its neighbors. In 2021 Saudi Arabia lifted the blockade on Qatar that was held for 43 months (Ulrichsen 2021). The blockade covered the air, land, and sea making it effectively impossible to create dialogue and cooperation between the two countries. The blockade started in 2017 due to Qatar trying to gain influence over Saudi Arabia and UAE specifically in Egypt, Libya, and Tunisia after the 2011 Arab Spring (Ulrichsen 2021).

The blockade ended up failing, not drawing up any significant economic or political support (Ulrichsen 2021). Qatar being brought back into the fold in the GCC will help create more cooperation as a whole for a shift to move into different markets in the world other than oil. Saudi Arabia created the Red Sea Council in hopes to bring together countries surrounding the Red Sea. It is a push for more multilateralism in the MENA region. The council consists of Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Jordan, Eritrea, Yemen, Sudan, Djibouti, and Somalia (Custers 2021). However, there have been some hiccups following the exclusion of some countries like Ethiopia, Somaliland, Turkey, and the UAE.

The main focus of the council is to address stability, though Saudi Arabia seems to only be focused on security issues, leading some to think their goals are actually very narrow minded. With Saudi Arabia’s focus on economic development they are trying to start up dialogues with Iran again after silence between the two for several years. This helps create less geopolitical risk involved when trying to expand into other markets in the region, also there could be a chance where they may start spending less on the military if there is decrease in threats surrounding them.

If Saudi Arabia is not focused or wasting energy on regional tensions due to rivals, then it can focus more on its own economic projects like Vision 2035. Reopening embassies can help create a path to more economic trade partnerships with Iran later down the line, helping strengthen other export sectors they have. A predictable region makes it easier to see the options you have to help move away from the rentier model. Mega projects are a big part of Saudi Arabia’s plan to create a more stable economy. Therefore, the state every couple of years comes up with a new plan for a huge project, usually a city.

In 2017 Suadi Arabia looked to start building an entertainment destination called Qiddiya. On the Website for Qiddiya it states it is “where thrilling entertainment & sports meet cultural experiences” (Qiddiya Investment Company n.d.). It looks as if this is an attempt to strengthen social institutions while simultaneously diversifying into the tourism sector. Creating a so-called third space for its own citizens through more recreational buildings. In turn this helps create more job opportunities with its aims of housing over 500,000 people, hopefully for Suadis and not more migrant workers. It is particularly more focused on tourism due to its key location being 40 minutes from a well-used airport called King Khalid Airport.

The goal of Qiddiya is said to be “to create a vibrant society, a thriving economy, and an ambitious nation.” (Qiddiya Investment Company n.d.). Another mega city being planned is called NEOM. This city was announced in 2021. The hope of NEOM was to build a city that is one hundred percent renewable, stating to have “no roads, cars or emissions” (NEOM n.d.). The goal of NEOM is just like Qiddiya, is to improve the social institutions of the state and create more jobs for the people there. Now both of these cities are still being either planned or built but the goal and hope of these cities is the same.

It is to improve the quality of life for Saudis, boost tourism, and to create more international partnerships. The Saudi Pro League, Saudi Arabia’s own soccer league, is being invested in more by the state. They are accomplishing this by offering lucrative contracts to world renowned players like Christaino Ronaldo, who plays for Al-Kassr (Pendharkar 2024). This push has brought widespread attention from millions, if not billions, of soccer fans. In the 2023 transfer window Saudi Arabia spent 875 million dollars on getting foreign players to their league (Pendharkar 2024). Their main goal from this is to increase global viewership of their league which, in turn, helps create higher profits and helps strengthen other sectors to fulfill their goal of diversification of their economy.

They are already seeing major profits from their efforts with a 650% increase in revenue within the “first month of the 2023/2024 season” (Pendharkar 2024). Esports is getting bigger and bigger each year as more people join the gaming world. Saudi Arabia wants to jump on this while it is still climbing. Through strategic investments and making gaming more a part of Saudi Arabia’s society, it may become one the biggest esport hubs in the world. The country is expected to make 13 billion USD by 2030 (Al-Sarraj 2025). With an investment into the gaming sector of 38 billion, there is hope that the outcome will be over 35,000 new jobs.

The Esports sector also gives Saudis a chance to build up their skills in technology and media (Al-Sarraj 2025). In addition, the majority of Saudi’s population is under 35. This creates a great opportunity for many young Saudis to develop skills related to the tech sector, one of the highest growing markets in the world. In a sense, the jump into Esports is helping foster a knowledge-based economy. To make things seem even brighter in this sector, a large portion of Saudi’s participate in gaming making it already a popular commodity in the country meaning there is so much room for this sector to grow. Of course there are challenges along the way, but with a focus on fixing these hiccups this seems to be something that will stick for a long time.

Qatar’s Economic Diversity Strategies

The World Cup was a significant stride in the diversification of Qatar’s economy. When Qatar received the bid for the World Cup in 2010 this accelerated investments into the country for infrastructure, technology, and tourism. It allowed them to have an opportunity to increase their international business partnerships. In the ten years leading up to the World Cup in Qatar, there was around 200-300 billion dollars invested into the country allowing them to start planned projects like underground transportation systems, hotels, stadiums, railways, a port, and stadiums. (Bibolov et al. 2024). They were able to start diversifying money into areas that were non-oil related.

It created significant transformations in their urban landscape. The spending Qatar did on their infrastructure through these investments helped create significant output growth in non-hydrocarbon sectors leading up to the World Cup. This infrastructure they have built will be lasting and the increased global visibility gained from the soccer event will lead to Qatar propped up more on the international stage (Bibolov et al. 2024). The specific sector that was targeted through all of this was the sports sector, which led to social institutions strengthening overall. Doha is the center for infrastructure investment when it comes to Qatar.

The majority of the population in Qatar lives in Doha and it is also the most visited place for international tourists. This is the place where the state gets to make a great first impression on outsiders. Trade is also huge in the city helping collect revenue for the country. The tourism sector and trade sector of Qatar have attempted to be upgraded by expansion of the Hamad International Airport and the creation of Hamad Port. Qatar’s expansion to their international airport increased its capacity significantly of people who could come to the airport and visit the state.

Their ultimate goal is to reach 70 million passengers per year. What did they do to expand the airport? They wanted to add more terminal space, so they created a huge indoor garden called the ORCHARD, and a large water feature. There was also a huge food/retail section of the airport that was expanded (“Hamad International Airport Terminal Expansion, Doha,” n.d.). The goal of these expansions is to modernize their airport and increase its reputation as a beautiful destination spot. They wanted to integrate new expansions into the airport that seemed seamless and increase a great first impression so more tourists would come visit, helping to increase the tourism sector.

Hamad Port is one of the largest ports in the Middle East. It is well positioned to be a regional shipping center for the GCC, allowing Qatar to rely on something other than the oil markets for income (“Hamad Port, Qatar,” 2023). They have expanded by adding three new shipping container terminals. This helps them import and export more products and increase trade interactions, giving them a base to start entering other high growth markets. This will increase interaction with more and more countries leading to boosting international business partners. Road and railway improvements have also helped connect the port all together and to other parts of the country.

This infrastructure improvement has helped create more efficiency to their trading sectors. With better transportation infrastructure surrounding the port there could be more specialized economic sectors that grow near the area (“Hamad Port, Qatar,” 2023). Increasing variety in economic activity through infrastructure improvements is the main goal of Qatar trying to move away from the rentier model. The expansion of Hamad Port could strengthen institutions like their legal and economic systems due to becoming more integrated with the rest of the world and playing a more substantial role in global groups like the United Nations and World Trade Organization.

The smart city initiatives in Qatar are helping to create more foreign investment through business partnerships. Lusail City is one of the most prominent smart cities, at the moment in Qatar, that is bringing in foreign capital and attention from companies. Lusail city’s goal isn't necessarily to move away from oil and gas, but to capitalize on the markets while they are still generating good revenue. The city offers a wide range of offices that manage the “responsible exploitation” of oil and gas (Adalbi, Ferwati, Ahmad, and Lee 2022). This helps create an attractive market that can bring in more foreign funding.

Boulevard street in Lusail city is supposedly going to be the largest “integrated commercial street” in the Gulf region (Adalbi, Ferwati, Ahmad, and Lee 2022). This will lead to more local investors. The city wants to develop more digital infrastructure like telecommunications and technological advancements for more public services (Adalbi, Ferwati, Ahmad, and Lee 2022). With high population growth in the region there is a higher need for urban expansion. Qatar has created a program called TASMU (Smart Qatar Program). This program is meant to apply technology in the state to help boost tourism and increase the quality of life in the country (Adalbi, Ferwati, Ahmad, and Lee 2022).

These improvements to the country using Lusail City will help strengthen social intentions like healthcare and entertainment. This will lead to an overall happier population while transitioning away from the rentier model. In addition, it increases funding towards the country, bringing more involvement with international companies. Education City in Qatar holds many college campuses to help strengthen the educational partnerships with other countries. Carnegie Mellon, Texas A&M, and HEC in Paris have Qatar campuses that are located in Education city. The area also holds almost all of Qatar’s research institutions. Having all these educational systems in one place helps foster learning and generate skilled workers who may end up deciding to live in Qatar after their studies.

This helps create a knowledge-based economy. This strengthening of the educational systems will lead to the R&D sector in Qatar to thrive adding to their diversification efforts. This is due to them finding other markets to join and maybe different products to sell through the help of the expansion of their research and development sectors.

U.S and China Relations with Saudi Arabia

For Saudi Arabia to be able to achieve their Vision 2030 plan in a reasonable timeframe they must rely on their foreign relations. China and the United States are powerful allies that can help diversify their economy. The United States has had a long partnership with Saudi Arabia though it has been shaky at times due to 9/11, the war in Yemen, and conversations starting up with Iran. There has been a growing relationship with China due the country's high investment in Chinese markets. China has also been discovered helping build infrastructure in Saudi Arabia.

Saudi Arabia knows that these two world powers are where to look in their time of need and they are utilizing the most from these partnerships. China, in the past decade, has been getting involved majorly with Saudi Arabia's needs of diversification. China has aligned itself to help nourish Saudi’s Vision 2030 project. China has had the BRI since 2013 however did not effectively help the country until MBS became Crown Prince in 2015 (Alghannam 2025). This is because Salman was more concerned than his predecessor about the future of the company and acknowledged that an economy based on oil will never be a long-lasting stable economy.

This led to MBS working closer with countries that had a higher variety of markets to be a part of. The BRI, known as the Belt and Road Initiative, is an economic infrastructure project that's main goal is to connect China to the rest of the world through seaports, railways, highways, energy pipelines, and industrial zones (Alghannam 2025). This helps build up China’s economy by strengthening ties to more countries all throughout the world. This also benefits the countries who are a part of the BRI because they get infrastructure built that can be used to trade more and increase production of their export sectors, which would increase their revenue.

In 2016, the Ambassador to Saudi Arabia from China named Li Huaxin, stated that it is extremely important that China’s BRI and Saudi Arabia’s Vision 2030 interact more with each other and how it would create massive benefits for them both. When MBS visited China, Xi stated that he would back their plan to help Vision 2030 and was committed to that (Alghannam 2025). The High-Level Joint Committee helped facilitate this cooperation between the BRI and Vision 2030. It helped by creating subcommittees that would focus more on investing in Saudi’s infrastructure and export sectors.

This led to 9 billion dollars being invested into Saudi Arabia between 2019 to 2024 (Alghannam 2025). Sectors like green energy, IT, cloud services, transport, construction, and manufacturing were all focused on China's investment into Saudi Arabia. China creating more partnerships with Saudi Arabia slowly takes away an ally from the U.S. Weakening the United States’ global power is always a plus for the Chinese government. The United States has had a long-lasting relationship with Saudi Arabia for decades now. However, there have been a few bumps in the road when it comes to that relationship.

When 9/11 happened there were reports found that some Saudi officials funded Al-Qaeda leading to a weakened relationship with the U.S. Saudi Arabia also had a war in Yemen which led to many human right violations leading the U.S to have to distance themselves a bit. Most recently Saudi Arabia began starting relationships up again with Iran after a no contact relationship for seven years. Iran being one of the United States’ enemies may create tension if their ally is becoming friends with them. Nonetheless through all this America and Saudi Arabia have stayed a strong alliance.

The U.S is trying to help Saudi Arabia diversify its economy by creating more business partnerships with Saudi Arabia, leading them to be in more high growth markets (U.S. Chamber of Commerce et al. 2017). With Saudi Arabia interacting with more international business partners, they can learn more about how to have successful business strategies and prop up their economy by making their own sectors more effective. This is called knowledge transfer, and it is a very effective sign of a country gaining more international business partnerships. U.S energy companies are partnering up with Saudi’s energy companies like Saudi Aramco (U.S.

Chamber of Commerce et al. 2017). A result of this, is that U.S oil companies are helping Saudi oil companies start using more carbon capture and storage methods. This helps make oil production and burning fossil fuels less dirty. These leads countries striving for sustainability to be politically comfortable with purchasing oil from Saudi Arabia. This lets the state capitalize on the oil they have. The U.S must keep a strong relation with Saudi Arabia while they are going through with their Vision 2030 plan or they may rely more heavily on China.

U.S and China Relations with Qatar

The U.S for a while now has been Qatar’s number one ally in the international stage. They have always been to Qatar’s aid when it comes to advancing their society. They have helped them by strengthening economic ties, creating security cooperation, and through diplomatic partnerships. With Qatar’s need to strengthen other export sectors, the U.S will invest even more into the country to help move away from the rentier model that it has been relying on for years. Qatar, on the other hand, is looking towards Chinese markets due to their high growth. This could lead to a huge growth in the other sectors of their economy.

China is Qatar’s third largest trading partner and the second largest source of imports (Chaziza 2020). In 2018 more than 14 China owned companies are working out of Qatar. This business partnership helps Qatar have more sources of income and motivates China to invest more in Qatar since they have businesses there. Qatar is also positioned as a key spot in the gulf that would benefit the BRI extensively in its mission to connect China economically throughout the world (Chaziza 2020). This gives Qatar the chance to benefit heavily from China’s BRI and gain many investments in their infrastructure to help build up their social institutions and economic institutions.

Just how the BRI is trying to cooperate with Saudi Arabia’s Vision 2030, the same is happening with the BRI and Qatar’s National Vision 2030. They want to cooperate more so they can make Qatar’s vision come to fruition. Qatar was also an early member of the Asian Infrastructure Investment Bank and established a Chinese yuan clearing center in Doha (Chaziza 2020). This helps Qatar and China gain more global power and economic influence in the world. It ensures that Qatar will be a regional financial hub. When it comes to energy, China is a huge importer of Qatar's natural liquified gas (Chaziza 2020).

This helps keep Qatar’s market stable and gives them income to help invest in other sectors. The United States is Qatar’s largest foreign investor and largest trading partner. There are over 850 U.S companies that are partnered with Qatar with their total economic relationship exceeding 200 billion in 2019 (U.S.-Qatar Business Council 2022). The international partnerships help create more economic opportunities by Qatar having more export markets to work with. With the United States being extremely active in trading with Qatar, the “two countries trade over 6.3 billion dollars in goods annually” (U.S.-Qatar Business Council 2022).

This is partially due to a trade agreement called the Trade and Investment Framework Agreement that was signed in 2004 (U.S.-Qatar Business Council 2022). Education city holds many U.S campuses there (U.S.-Qatar Business Council 2022). This allows the U.S to send students over to study in Qatar by using their own college campuses. This leads to Qatar being exposed to a larger audience that is highly educated. This also gives a chance for Qataris to go to U.S universities without leaving their country. This helps foster a knowledge-based economy, which helps strengthen social institutions like education. United States tech companies are also playing a role in Qatar’s diversification efforts by helping build up smart cities like Lusail and Msheireb Downtown Doha (U.S.-Qatar Business Council 2022).

What Does The Future Hold

Qatar after the World Cup has quieted down a bit but there are plans to get themselves in more high growth markets. The creation of The Follow Up Committee has helped plan out strategic economic decisions to prop up Qatar on the international stage. This will hopefully lead to more international investments Qatar and more business partnerships. Qatar is also trying to enter high-growth digital markets in China and India. This will take time to see if anything comes to fruition with this but there could be big economic rewards to the shift in focus from Europe to Asia.

It seems that Qatar is working with anyone who can help them and with their economy starting to slow down since the World Cup, they are starting to wonder who will still be in their corner for the long run while they move away from the rentier model and strengthen the economy. Saudi Arabia is pushing themselves into any market available and having some great success with it. Esports are a very new venture in the world right now and Saudi Arabia found themselves leading it. This could become a pattern for the state, jumping into these growing markets and finding themselves in the front of it.

With ExxonMobile helping Saudi Arabia with carbon capture and storage (CCS), U.S companies are finding ways to get Saudi Arabia to capitalize on the oil it has. How long will CCS be able to keep countries buying from them? There is not a definite answer but for now it is working. With Saudi’s conversation start-ups with Iran there could end up being more stabilization in the region but first the results of the dialogues need to be revealed, and the end game must be reached. Things could crumble quickly and so only time will tell if these de-escalation methods with Iran will help.

In 2034 Saudi Arabia will host the World Cup and it will benefit the country the same way it benefited Qatar, by accelerating their infrastructure investments and changing the landscape of the country. Lastly Saudi Arabia has started the Women Empowerment Budget Initiative, which aims to promote more girls to go to school and create a more inclusive work environment in hopes to diversify the job market more. These two gulf states seem to have plenty of ideas to keep them afloat for the foreseeable future but there are always issues that can arise. They seem to wel, aware of the danger of basing their economy solely on one resource whose market is supposed to stagnate in the next ten years.

The mere fact they know of this means that they will find a way to not lose the income it is taking in. Using the United States and China is a smart move for them both. Playing both sides will ultimately give them the resources they need to survive the Dutch disease and build up their other export sectors. However, they must be careful because if they stray too much to one side they could lose the other country completely. As long as they both focus on the development of their social, political, economic, and legal institutions and keep looking for new markets to join, they may not complete their vision plans but they will have successfully moved away from the political and economic system called rentierism.
